Foundations

Grounded in clinical epidemiology

This module is built on Understanding and Conducting Clinical Research — A Clinical Epidemiology Approach by a Clinician for Clinicians (Chew BH, UPM, 2019; ISBN 978-967-960-450-4) and the AI tool manuals by the same author. It also integrates the Framework for AI Fluency (Dakan & Feller, 2025) as adopted by the Anthropic Academy.

What clinical epidemiology provides

Clinical epidemiology is the science of making clinical measurement relevant, valid, and precise. It equips researchers with principles of study design, strategies to prevent bias and confounding, sample size reasoning, effect estimate interpretation, and reporting standards.

Chew BH. An Illustrated Version. UPM; 2019. DOI: 10.7759/cureus.4112

What AI tools provide

AI tools accelerate specific tasks across the research lifecycle: literature searching and synthesis, proposal drafting and critique, SPSS/R syntax generation and debugging, statistical output interpretation, and manuscript drafting, editing and compliance checking.

AI supplements clinical judgment — it does not replace it. The researcher remains the expert pilot; AI is the thinking partner.

Three epidemiologic design principles

Every clinical research is planned across three interlocking designs:

  • Theoretical design — qualitative or quantitative; primary or secondary; causal or non-causal
  • Data collection design — experimental or non-experimental; sampling or census; time features
  • Statistical design — pre-specified, pre-determined, exploratory analyses

Four categories of clinical research

Based on Grobbee & Hoes classification, all clinical research belongs to one of four epidemiologic categories:

  • Diagnostic — cross-sectional; odds ratio; gold standard required
  • Prognostic — longitudinal; hazard ratio; calibration and discrimination
  • Interventional — RCT; risk ratio; intention-to-treat analysis
  • Etiologic — cohort/case-control; relative risk; incidence

Research classification — the full picture

Clinical research can be classified in many ways. The classification used in this module — based on Grobbee & Hoes, as presented by Chew (2019) — maps directly onto the questions clinicians and patients ask in practice. Two broader classification systems also exist alongside it.

Figure 1. Fundamental classification of clinical researches (Chew BH, 2019)

Other classification systems

Stokes / Chalmers: Based on axes of knowledge generation and application — pure basic research, inspired basic research, pure applied research.

Common labels: Basic research · Clinical research · Health Care (Services) research · Health Systems (Policy) research · Educational research

Also commonly encountered

Cross-sectional · Retrospective cohort · Prospective cohort · Mixed-method · Quasi-experimental — and others. These terms describe data collection design features rather than epidemiologic categories, and are best understood within the three-design framework.

Research types — definitions and real examples

The terms below are used throughout clinical and health research. Definitions are drawn from authoritative sources — WHO, the Dictionary of Epidemiology (Porta, 2014), Grobbee & Hoes, and Chew BH (2019) — and cross-referenced with the four-category clinical research framework above.

1 · Clinical Research

Research conducted on or with human participants (or human-derived material) to generate knowledge applicable to improving health outcomes, clinical practice, or health policy. Clinical research subsumes all four epidemiologic categories — diagnostic, prognostic, interventional, and etiologic — and encompasses both quantitative and qualitative designs.

Chew BH. An Illustrated Version. UPM; 2019 · WHO definition of clinical research

2 · Medical Research

A broader term that encompasses clinical research plus basic research conducted in laboratory settings (in vitro, in vivo animal studies, molecular and cell biology), with the intention of advancing medical knowledge. Not all medical research involves human participants directly — but clinical research always does. In Malaysia, the term "medical research" is used in the NMRR registration system and by the MOH MREC to mean research involving human subjects in health and medical contexts.

Declaration of Helsinki (2024) · Porta M. Dictionary of Epidemiology. 5th ed. Oxford; 2014

3 · Biomedical Research

Research that applies the biological sciences to the understanding, prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of disease. Includes molecular biology, genomics, biochemistry, physiology, pharmacology, and related disciplines — from cell culture to first-in-human trials. Biomedical research is the broadest category; it includes all of medical research and extends to non-clinical laboratory science. In grant taxonomy (NIH, Wellcome), biomedical research encompasses basic, translational, and clinical research along the bench-to-bedside continuum.

NIH definition of biomedical research · Stokes DE. Pasteur's Quadrant. 1997

4 · Qualitative Research

Research that seeks to understand meaning, experience, context, and social processes through the collection and analysis of non-numerical data (words, images, observations). Designs include phenomenology, grounded theory, ethnography, thematic analysis, and case study. Qualitative research asks "how?" and "why?" — not "how many?" It uses purposive sampling, reaches data saturation rather than statistical power, and evaluates quality through trustworthiness criteria (Lincoln & Guba, 1985) rather than validity and reliability statistics.

Moser A, Korstjens I. Eur J Gen Pract. 2017–2018 (series) · Lincoln YS, Guba EG. Naturalistic Inquiry. Sage; 1985

5 · Quantitative Research

Research that generates or analyses numerical data using statistical methods to measure, describe, or test relationships between variables. Includes experimental designs (RCTs) and non-experimental designs (cross-sectional, cohort, case-control, registry studies). Quantitative research asks "how much?", "how often?", and "is there an association?" It requires pre-specified hypotheses, adequate sample size (power calculation), and reports effect estimates with confidence intervals and p-values.

Grobbee DE, Hoes AW. Clinical Epidemiology. Jones & Bartlett; 2009 · Chew BH. UPM; 2019

6 · Primary Research

Research in which the investigator collects original, first-hand data directly from participants or the environment — rather than using pre-existing datasets. Includes surveys, clinical trials, cohort studies, qualitative interviews, and observational studies. Primary research requires ethics approval for data collection, participant recruitment, and informed consent.

Porta M. Dictionary of Epidemiology. Oxford; 2014 · Chew BH. UPM; 2019

7 · Secondary Research

Research that analyses pre-existing data originally collected for another purpose — including clinical records, registries (NMRR, hospital EMR), administrative databases, published studies (systematic reviews, meta-analyses), and biobanks. Secondary research typically requires ethics review but not individual participant recruitment. Advantages include large sample sizes and long follow-up; disadvantages include limited control over data quality, completeness, and variable definitions.

Porta M. Dictionary of Epidemiology. Oxford; 2014 · Higgins JPT et al. Cochrane Handbook. 2019

8 · Diagnostic Research

Research that characterises a defined population at a single point in time — classifying, describing, or measuring a phenomenon cross-sectionally without requiring a comparator or gold-standard reference test. Diagnostic research uses odds ratios (ORs) as its primary measure of association. Prevalence studies (counting existing cases at a point in time) and Knowledge–Attitude–Practice (KAP) studies are sub-types of diagnostic research. Psychometric studies — validating a questionnaire or measurement instrument in a target population — also belong here, as they characterise the measurement properties of an instrument in a cross-sectional sample. Note: Although prevalence studies yield a prevalence proportion, they do not meet the statistical rigour required of Diagnostic Test Research (see item 9 below), which requires sensitivity, specificity, and a gold-standard comparator.

Grobbee DE, Hoes AW. Clinical Epidemiology. 2009 · Chew BH. UPM; 2019

9 · Diagnostic Test Research

Research that evaluates the accuracy of a diagnostic test (index test) against a reference standard (gold standard) in a cross-sectional sample. Requires: an index test, a reference standard applied to the same participants, and calculation of sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value (PPV), negative predictive value (NPV), and likelihood ratios. Reported using the STARD (Standards for Reporting of Diagnostic Accuracy Studies) checklist. Diagnostic test research is a specific, technically demanding sub-type of diagnostic research — it goes well beyond prevalence studies in its design requirements and statistical analysis.

Bossuyt PM et al. STARD 2015. BMJ. 2015;351:h5527 · Chew BH. UPM; 2019

10 · Prognostic Research

Research that determines the natural course, outcomes, or predictors of outcomes in people who already have a condition, exposure, or risk factor — followed forward in time. Primary measures include hazard ratios (Cox regression), incidence rates, and cumulative incidence. Cohort studies (prospective and retrospective) are the main design; incidence studies (counting new cases in a defined period) and longitudinal point-prevalence studies (repeated cross-sectional measures over time) may contribute to prognostic evidence. Prognostic models (clinical prediction rules) are evaluated using calibration and discrimination statistics (AUC, C-statistic, Brier score).

Grobbee DE, Hoes AW. Clinical Epidemiology. 2009 · Moons KGM et al. TRIPOD. Ann Intern Med. 2015

11 · Intervention Research

Research that tests the effect of an active intervention — a drug, device, behavioural programme, surgical procedure, educational intervention, or health system change — on an outcome. The randomised controlled trial (RCT) is the gold standard for intervention research because randomisation is the only design feature that controls both measured and unmeasured confounding. Includes feasibility trials, pilot RCTs, explanatory and pragmatic trials, cluster RCTs, and stepped-wedge designs. Primary measures: risk ratio (RR), risk difference (RD), number needed to treat (NNT), analysed by intention-to-treat (ITT).

Chew BH. UPM; 2019 · Schulz KF et al. CONSORT 2010. BMJ. 2010;340:c332

12 · Etiologic Research

Research that investigates the causes or determinants of disease — why some people develop a condition and others do not. Etiologic research requires a temporal sequence (exposure precedes outcome), an association of sufficient strength, a dose–response relationship, and biological plausibility. Study designs that can contribute to etiologic evidence include: case-control studies (efficient for rare diseases; measure odds ratio); prospective cohort studies (strongest observational design; relative risk); retrospective cohort studies (using existing records; relative risk); and interventional studies (RCTs provide the strongest causal evidence when randomisation breaks the confounding chain). Primary measure: relative risk (RR) or hazard ratio (HR); in case-control designs, odds ratio (OR) as an approximation of RR.

Porta M. Dictionary of Epidemiology. Oxford; 2014 · Chew BH. UPM; 2019 · Grobbee & Hoes. 2009

Mixed methods and multimethod research — when more than one approach is used

Both mixed methods and multimethod research use more than one approach within a single project, but they differ fundamentally in when and how the approaches interact — not simply in which data types are combined.

Mixed methods research (Creswell & Plano Clark)

Deliberately integrates qualitative and quantitative data — at the design, collection, analysis, or interpretation stage — so that the combined understanding exceeds what either approach alone could produce. Integration is the defining feature, not merely the co-existence of methods.

Designs: Convergent · Explanatory sequential · Exploratory sequential · Embedded

Multimethod research (Morse, 2003)

Each method is conducted as a complete, independent, standalone study. The methods do not interact during data collection or analysis — they are only brought together at the final interpretation stage. Critically, this can be intra-paradigmatic (QUAL + qual, or QUAN + quan) or inter-paradigmatic (QUAL + quan), as long as independence is maintained throughout. The defining criterion is methodological independence, not paradigm membership.

Example: a standalone RCT (QUAN) + a standalone phenomenological study (QUAL) — each complete on its own; results merged only in the final discussion

The key distinction is independence, not paradigm. Mixed methods research requires active integration across the design. Multimethod research requires each component to stand alone as a publishable study — the paradigm combination (intra- or inter-) is secondary to this structural independence. A multimethod project using QUAL + quan is not "mixed methods" because the two components are never integrated during the research process itself.

Four integration strategies in mixed methods (Creswell & Plano Clark, 2018): Connecting — one dataset guides selection of participants for the next phase; Building — findings from one phase develop the instrument for the next; Merging — quantitative and qualitative data are directly compared for triangulation; Embedding — both data types integrated at multiple points within the same study.

Creswell JW, Plano Clark VL. Designing and Conducting Mixed Methods Research. 3rd ed. SAGE; 2018. ISBN 978-1-5063-3104-3 · Morse JM. Principles of mixed and multimethod research design. In: Tashakkori A, Teddlie C, eds. Handbook of Mixed Methods in Social & Behavioral Research. SAGE; 2003. doi:10.4324/9781315424538 · Chew BH. An Illustrated Version. UPM; 2019. Tables 2–3 · Chew BH. Proposal Manual. UPM; 2025.

Implementation research and hybrid trials

Once an intervention is proven efficacious, a second and equally important question arises: how do we get it into routine practice — and keep it there? Implementation research addresses this question. Hybrid effectiveness-implementation trials bridge the gap between efficacy and implementation by combining both questions in a single study, accelerating the journey from evidence to practice.

Implementation research — what it is and why it matters

Implementation research is the scientific study of methods to promote the systematic uptake of research findings and other evidence-based practices into routine clinical and public health practice, to improve the quality and effectiveness of health services (Eccles & Mittman, Implementation Science, 2006;1:1). It fills a critical gap: the knowledge-to-action gap — the well-documented failure of evidence-based interventions to reach patients at scale, often taking 17 or more years from trial evidence to routine clinical practice (Balas & Boren, 2000).

Definition and scope

Implementation research asks: Why do evidence-based interventions fail to be adopted, delivered with fidelity, or sustained? What strategies overcome these barriers? For whom, in which settings, and under what conditions does an implementation strategy work? It is applied across health systems, community health programmes, and clinical practice — from primary care to hospital settings to public health policy.

The Proctor implementation outcomes taxonomy

Proctor et al. (2011) proposed eight distinct implementation outcomes that must be distinguished from clinical treatment outcomes:

  • Acceptability — stakeholders find it agreeable
  • Adoption — intent or decision to use it
  • Appropriateness — perceived fit to the setting
  • Feasibility — can be carried out in that context
  • Fidelity — delivered as intended
  • Penetration — reach within an eligible population
  • Sustainability — maintained over time
  • Implementation cost — cost of implementation activities

Key frameworks used in implementation research

Implementation strategies — the 'how' of implementation

A determinant framework identifying barriers and facilitators across five domains: intervention characteristics, outer setting, inner setting, characteristics of individuals, and implementation process. Used to diagnose why implementation succeeded or failed and to tailor strategies accordingly.

Damschroder et al. Implement Sci. 2009;4:50

How implementation research differs from clinical research

Clinical research asks: Does this intervention work? (efficacy) or Does it work in real-world conditions? (effectiveness). Implementation research asks: How do we get it into practice? With what fidelity? At what cost? Sustained by whom? These are different questions requiring different outcomes (Proctor's taxonomy above), different designs (stepped-wedge, cluster RCT, hybrid trials), and different frameworks (CFIR, RE-AIM). Both are necessary — an intervention that works in a trial but fails to reach patients at scale has limited public health value.

Effectiveness-implementation hybrid trials — bridging the evidence-to-practice gap

A hybrid effectiveness-implementation trial takes a dual focus a priori — combining a question about clinical effectiveness with a question about how to implement the intervention. The approach was codified by Curran et al. (2012), whose typology has since been cited over 2,000 times and is now explicitly required by NIH, PCORI, and many international funders. As of 2012, it was proposed that "in the future, all good trials will be hybrid in some way."

The three hybrid types (Curran et al. 2012)

Type Primary focus Secondary focus Use when
Type 1 Testing clinical effectiveness of an intervention (primary) Observing implementability — barriers, facilitators, context (secondary) Intervention has limited direct effectiveness evidence; understanding real-world feasibility is needed for future implementation
Type 2 Dual co-primary focus: clinical effectiveness AND implementation outcomes Testing implementation strategy simultaneously with effectiveness trial Intervention has indirect effectiveness evidence; implementation strategy needs piloting alongside clinical testing
Type 3 Testing an implementation strategy — adoption, fidelity, penetration, sustainability (primary) Gathering effectiveness data on the clinical intervention (secondary) Intervention is proven effective but remains underused; need to find the best strategy to scale it up

Why hybrid trials matter for clinical researchers

Traditional clinical trials test efficacy or effectiveness in controlled or semi-controlled settings — they answer "does it work?" but not "will clinicians use it, at what cost, and for how long?" Hybrid trials simultaneously generate both types of knowledge, producing more rapid translational gains, more effective implementation strategies, and more useful information for health system decision-makers. For grant applicants: NIH has explicitly directed hybrid proposals to specific review panels and actively funds all three types under multiple mechanisms.

One of the most widely cited published examples of a Type 2 hybrid trial.

AI Fluency Framework

Understanding AI for research — what you need to know

AI is already transforming how research is conducted, written, and published. You do not need to be a computer scientist to use it effectively — but you do need to understand what AI actually is, what it reliably does, and where its limits lie. This section builds the mental model that prepares you to work confidently and responsibly with AI throughout both parts of this module.

What is AI? What is an LLM?

Artificial intelligence (AI) is the field of computer science concerned with building systems that perform tasks that would normally require human intelligence — understanding language, recognising patterns, making decisions, generating content. Modern AI is not programmed with rules: it learns patterns from large amounts of data.

A Large Language Model (LLM) is the specific type of AI that powers tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. LLMs are trained on vast amounts of text — books, websites, research papers, code — and learn to predict the most statistically likely continuation of any given input. They are extraordinarily good at generating fluent, relevant, contextually appropriate text. They do not "look things up" in real time (unless connected to a search tool), do not "know" facts the way humans do, and can generate confident-sounding text that is factually wrong — a phenomenon called hallucination.

Five properties that shape how you use every LLM: (1) Context window — the LLM only "sees" what you put in your current conversation; it has no memory of past sessions unless you re-paste context. (2) Probabilistic output — two identical prompts can produce different outputs; there is no single "correct" answer. (3) No real-time knowledge — the model was trained up to a cutoff date; recent events, new guidelines, or updated statistics require web search tools. (4) No intrinsic accuracy — fluency is not reliability; always verify numbers, citations, and clinical claims. (5) Steerability — you control quality through prompt design; short, concrete, verifiable instructions produce more reliable outputs than long, abstract ones.

General-purpose vs specialised AI tools for research

Not all AI tools are the same. Understanding the difference prevents both under-use and misuse.

General-purpose LLMs

ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek. Broad capability across any domain. Best for: drafting, editing, code generation, prompt-based analysis, synthesis, and writing assistance. Require strong prompting and output verification. Knowledge cutoff applies — no automatic access to new literature.

Specialised research tools

Elicit, Consensus, Perplexity, scite.ai, NotebookLM. Purpose-built for specific research tasks — literature search, evidence synthesis, citation verification. Retrieve from live databases. Reduce hallucination risk for factual retrieval. Trade breadth for reliability in their domain.

Use specialised tools for literature retrieval and citation verification; use general LLMs for drafting, synthesis, and writing. The two complement each other — neither replaces the other.

AI agents — what they are and why they matter for research

Beyond single-turn conversations, AI agents are systems where an LLM orchestrates sequences of actions — searching databases, running code, evaluating outputs, and iterating — to complete complex multi-step tasks with minimal human intervention. In March 2026, The AI Scientist (Lu et al., Nature 651:914–919) became the first agentic system to automate the full ML research cycle from idea generation through experiment, analysis, manuscript writing, and automated peer review, with a paper passing the first round of workshop review at a top-tier conference.

For health sciences researchers, three constraints mean end-to-end agentic automation does not currently apply: (1) human participants requiring ethics approval and informed consent; (2) patient data governed by privacy law and institutional governance; and (3) interpretive accountability — clinical decisions must be made by qualified professionals who can be held responsible. Agents can accelerate components (literature search, analysis coding, draft writing), but the researcher remains legally and ethically responsible for all outputs.

Meanwhile, even as AI accelerates the generation of findings, the academic publishing system remains a significant bottleneck — peer review taking 12–18 months, article processing charges of USD 5,000–11,000, and a reproducibility crisis where an estimated 50–90% of published findings may not replicate (Chew, JMIR 2026). AI tools solve the production problem, not the validation and trust problem.

Researcher training & preparedness — a non-negotiable prerequisite

No researcher should embark on clinical research without adequate training. Under the Malaysian Guideline for Good Clinical Practice (GCP), Fifth Edition 2026 (ICH E6(R3), MOH/S/ICR/74.26(GU)-e), Section 2.1 mandates that every investigator must have sufficient education, training, and experience to assume responsibility for the proper conduct of a clinical trial. A qualified physician or appropriately qualified healthcare professional must have overall medical responsibility for the trial. The GCP Fifth Edition — adapted from the 2025 ICH E6(R3) update — additionally emphasises a quality culture, proactive risk-based design, and competency with electronic and digital data systems.

GCP certification — mandatory for clinical trialists; strongly recommended for all clinical researchers

All principal investigators and sub-investigators involved in clinical trials (interventional studies with an investigational product) conducted in Malaysia are required to hold a current GCP certificate. The certificate is awarded upon completing a course accredited by the National Committee for Clinical Research (NCCR) / Institute for Clinical Research (ICR), Ministry of Health Malaysia. As of the Fifth Edition 2026, over 30,000 individuals in Malaysia have obtained GCP certification. Certificates must be renewed periodically (typically every 2–3 years) and must be available in the trial master file.

Important note: GCP certification is not a regulatory requirement for clinical researchers conducting observational, survey-based, qualitative, or secondary-data studies that do not involve an investigational product. However, the principles of GCP — ethical conduct, data integrity, participant protection, and documentation standards — remain fully applicable to all clinical research, regardless of design. Researchers conducting non-interventional clinical research are strongly encouraged to obtain GCP certification as a mark of professional preparedness.

Source: Malaysian Guideline for GCP, 5th Ed. 2026, §2.1; Guideline Scope (p. 2); ICR — icr.nih.gov.my

NMRR registration — required before recruitment begins

Every clinical study involving human participants in Malaysia must be registered with the National Medical Research Register (NMRR) before any recruitment commences. Prospective registration is required for ethics approval applications to MREC (MOH), JKEUPM, or other institutional ethics committees. NMRR registration documents the protocol, PI credentials, and confirms ethical oversight — it cannot be backdated after data collection begins.

nmrr.gov.my

Methodology training — required for all clinical researchers, regardless of study design

Whether or not GCP certification applies, every clinical researcher must be adequately trained in the specific methodology they intend to use before data collection begins. There is no substitute for formal training in the methods underpinning your research design. Ad hoc learning during a live study is a protocol deviation, an ethics concern, and a source of preventable bias. Training should be documented and, where possible, credentialled.

For quantitative & clinical trial researchers

  • GCP course (NCCR/ICR — mandatory for clinical trials)
  • Clinical epidemiology and biostatistics workshop
  • Sample size calculation and power analysis training
  • Research design and protocol development course
  • Systematic review and meta-analysis methodology (if applicable)
  • Data management and FAIR principles workshop
  • CONSORT / STROBE / PRISMA reporting standards

For qualitative & mixed-methods researchers (special attention required)

  • Formal qualitative methodology training (e.g., grounded theory, thematic analysis, IPA, phenomenology, ethnography) — approach-specific
  • Purposive and theoretical sampling principles
  • Interview and focus group facilitation skills workshop
  • Qualitative data analysis software training (NVivo, Atlas.ti, MAXQDA)
  • Reflexivity and positionality in qualitative inquiry
  • Rigour and trustworthiness criteria (Lincoln & Guba framework)
  • COREQ / SRQR / ENTREQ reporting standards for qualitative research

Qualitative researchers require particular attention. Qualitative methodology is frequently misunderstood as requiring less formal preparation than quantitative work. This is incorrect. Qualitative research demands a different — and equally rigorous — skillset: epistemological literacy, reflexive awareness, method-appropriate sampling, and approach-specific analysis techniques. A researcher trained only in quantitative methods should not conduct qualitative interviews and analysis without dedicated qualitative methodology training first. Many qualitative research errors (e.g., applying thematic analysis to data collected for grounded theory; misrepresenting saturation; inadequate reflexivity statements) arise directly from inadequate pre-study preparation.

AI literacy is now also a required preparedness domain. Elsevier's 2025 Researcher of the Future report (n=3,200+ researchers, 113 countries) found that while 58% of researchers now use AI tools in their work, only 27% believe they have adequate training in using AI and just 32% say their institution provides good AI governance. Research project team set-up — expertise, roles, and accountability

The research team is not assembled after the project begins — it is constituted as part of study design, before ethics submission. GCP §2.2 specifies that the investigator must have adequate resources, including sufficient number of qualified staff and adequate facilities for the foreseen duration of the trial. GCP §3.7 further requires sponsors to select investigators who are qualified by training, experience, and resources to conduct the trial. A well-structured research team ensures methodological rigour, ethical accountability, participant safety, and sustainable research output.

Essential team roles and required expertise

  • Principal Investigator (PI) — Qualified physician or senior researcher; overall responsibility for protocol, safety, and ethics compliance; GCP-certified
  • Sub-investigators / Co-investigators — Clinical or methodological expertise relevant to study design; GCP-certified and listed in trial master file
  • Biostatistician — Involved from sample size calculation through analysis plan and final statistical report; co-authorship commensurate with contribution
  • Clinical Research Coordinator (CRC) — Day-to-day trial management, data collection, participant follow-up; GCP-trained
  • Data manager — Ensures data integrity, FAIR compliance, audit trail maintenance

What GCP requires of team composition

GCP §2.2 (Resources) states the investigator must demonstrate that: adequate numbers of qualified staff are available; staff are trained in the protocol and GCP; and facilities for the expected duration of the trial are adequate. GCP §3.7 (Investigator selection) requires the sponsor to document that investigators have qualifications, time, and resources to conduct the trial before it begins.

The team composition must be declared in the ethics application, with CVs and GCP certificates of the PI and sub-investigators as mandatory attachments. Any change in key personnel during the trial must be communicated to the ethics committee.

Building an effective research team — evidence-based principles (Nature, 2022)

Pedersen CL (Nature Career Column, 2022) identifies that the most productive scientific collaborations rest on two orthogonal dimensions: competence alignment (does each collaborator bring expertise the team lacks?) and motivational alignment (do all members share the same goals and commitment levels?). Teams with high competence but low motivational alignment produce "misaligned partners"; high motivation but low competence yields "enthusiastic novices." The optimal team brings together complementary methodological expertise with aligned scientific motivation — a direct parallel to GCP's requirement for both qualification and adequate time commitment from each team member.

Pedersen CL. How to pick a great scientific collaborator. Nature. 2022. doi:10.1038/d41586-022-01323-9

Non-negotiable principles

Research integrity

Research integrity is the foundation on which all scientific knowledge rests. Without it, data cannot be trusted, findings cannot be replicated, and clinical decisions cannot be safely grounded in evidence. It is defined as adherence to professional values, standards, and practices of the research community — honesty, rigour, transparency, and accountability — in the design, conduct, analysis, and reporting of research. Integrity is not simply the absence of misconduct; it is an active, ongoing commitment to the trustworthiness of research at every stage of the research cycle.

Research misconduct — fabrication, falsification, and plagiarism (FFP)

The Office of Science and Technology Policy (OSTP) and the US Office of Research Integrity (ORI) define research misconduct as "fabrication, falsification, or plagiarism in proposing, performing, or reviewing research, or in reporting research results." These three acts — commonly referred to as FFP — represent the most serious violations of research integrity. To be classified as research misconduct under federal policy, actions must represent a significant departure from accepted practices, have been committed intentionally, knowingly, or recklessly, and be proven by a preponderance of evidence.

Fabrication

Making up data, results, or findings and recording or reporting them as if they were real. Includes inventing participant responses, laboratory measurements, or clinical outcomes that were never collected.

Falsification

Manipulating research materials, equipment, or processes, or changing or omitting data or results such that the research is not accurately represented. Includes selectively deleting inconvenient data points without justification.

Plagiarism

The appropriation of another person's ideas, processes, results, or words without giving appropriate credit. Includes copying text, figures, or data from published or unpublished work — including self-plagiarism of one's own previously published work.

Consequences of research misconduct — FFP is not merely a professional failing. It constitutes a fundamental breach of the social contract between science and society. Researchers found guilty of FFP may face retraction of published work, loss of funding, institutional sanctions, professional debarment, and in severe cases criminal liability. Every fabricated result that enters the literature can inform — and potentially harm — clinical practice until it is detected and retracted. The average time from publication to retraction is over four years.

Source: OSTP Federal Policy on Research Misconduct, December 2000 · ORI — Office of Research Integrity, US DHHS — ori.hhs.gov · NSF Responsible and Ethical Conduct of Research — nsf.gov

Questionable research practices (QRPs) — the broader spectrum

Research misconduct (FFP) represents only the most severe end of a far broader range of problematic behaviours. The UKRIO spectrum of Questionable Research Practices (Kolstoe, 2023) conceptualises research integrity failures as a continuum — from honest errors and sloppiness at one end, through to falsification, fabrication, and criminality at the other. This framing is important: it recognises that everyone involved in research may at times engage in QRPs, and that addressing them is therefore a community-wide responsibility, not a matter of policing a minority.

The QRP spectrum — left to right, from unintentional to deliberate

← Least severe

Honest errors & mistakes (methodology, statistical, data entry)

Misunderstandings

Genuine disagreements about data interpretation; inadequate training

Sloppiness & incompetence

Poor record-keeping; inadequate methodology; p-hacking; HARKing; cherry-picking

Intentional QRPs

Ghost authorship; gift authorship; salami slicing; duplicate publication; data manipulation

Most severe →

Fabrication, falsification, plagiarism, fraud, criminality

Common QRPs in clinical research

  • HARKing — Hypothesising After Results are Known; presenting a post-hoc hypothesis as if it were pre-specified
  • p-hacking — running multiple analyses until a p-value <0.05 is achieved without pre-registration
  • Cherry-picking — reporting only favourable outcomes or time points; outcome switching
  • Salami slicing — dividing one study's data into multiple publications for greater output
  • Gift authorship — listing authors who made no substantive contribution
  • Ghost authorship — omitting those who made significant contributions
  • Duplicate publication — submitting the same data to multiple journals simultaneously

Why QRPs matter — even the "minor" ones

Minor infringements on the left of the spectrum can escalate. A researcher who habitually cuts corners on record-keeping may, under competitive or financial pressure, move progressively toward the right — covering up mistakes rather than acknowledging them. Kolstoe (2023) emphasises that the appropriate response to QRPs depends on their position on the spectrum: methodological safeguards (strict protocols, open data, pre-registration) address the left; governance processes and sanctions address the right.

Humility is a key research virtue. Acknowledging and correcting errors is a marker of research integrity — not a weakness. How a researcher responds when a mistake is pointed out is more important than the fact that the mistake occurred.

What research integrity requires — the positive obligations

Integrity is not merely the avoidance of misconduct. It demands a set of active, ongoing behaviours that the research community has defined through international statements and codes of conduct. Three authoritative frameworks set out these positive obligations.

Singapore Statement on Research Integrity (2010)

The first international consensus on research integrity principles. Establishes four core principles: Honesty in all aspects of research · Accountability in the conduct of research · Professional courtesy and fairness · Good stewardship of research on behalf of others. And nine responsibilities covering integrity, adherence to regulations, appropriate methods, accurate records, open sharing, authorship, acknowledgement, peer review, and conflict-of-interest disclosure.

wcrif.org/singapore-statement · Available in Bahasa Malaysia

European Code of Conduct for Research Integrity — ALLEA (2023 Revised)

The primary standard for EU-funded research and a global model for institutional codes. Organised around four principles: Reliability (rigour in design, methodology, analysis, and reporting) · Honesty (transparent communication and conflict-of-interest declaration) · Respect (for participants, animals, environment, and colleagues) · Accountability (for research from conception to publication, data stewardship, and societal responsibility). The 2023 edition adds explicit requirements for research culture, GDPR compliance, and responsible AI use.

allea.org/code-of-conduct · Endorsed by the European Commission for all Horizon Europe projects

COPE Code of Conduct for Researchers

The Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE) establishes expectations for researchers at the publication stage: honest and complete reporting · ethical data management · appropriate authorship · disclosure of all conflicts of interest · responsible peer review · prompt correction of errors. COPE provides a suite of flowcharts for handling suspected misconduct in submitted and published papers, used by journals worldwide.

publicationethics.org

Hong Kong Principles for assessing researchers (Moher et al., PLOS Biology, 2020)

For research integrity to be sustainable, researchers must be rewarded for responsible practices — not just penalised for misconduct. Moher et al. (2020) argue that the dominant publish-or-perish metric system creates perverse incentives that push researchers toward QRPs. The Hong Kong Principles (HKPs), developed at the 6th World Conference on Research Integrity, propose five principles for how research institutions should assess and reward researchers: (1) Assess for responsible research practices — pre-registration, methodology transparency, data sharing; (2) Value transparent reporting — adherence to reporting guidelines (CONSORT, STROBE, PRISMA); (3) Value open research — open data, open materials, open access; (4) Value a diversity of research outputs — reviews, datasets, methods papers, not only high-impact journal articles; (5) Value all contributions — peer review, mentoring, outreach, and knowledge exchange. Implementation of the HKPs would create institutional incentives that align individual career advancement with research trustworthiness.

Moher D, Bouter L, Kleinert S et al. The Hong Kong Principles for assessing researchers: Fostering research integrity. PLOS Biol. 2020;18(7):e3000737. doi:10.1371/journal.pbio.3000737 · Open access (CC BY 4.0)

Montreal Statement on Research Integrity in Cross-Boundary Collaborations (2013)

As research becomes increasingly collaborative across institutions, disciplines, and countries, integrity obligations extend across the collaboration as a whole. The Montreal Statement — a 20-point companion to the Singapore Statement, developed at the 3rd World Conference on Research Integrity — outlines the responsibilities of all collaborating partners: mutual agreement on roles and responsibilities from the outset; open discussion of customary practices and assumptions; prompt resolution of conflicts; agreed authorship and acknowledgement standards (with full recognition for junior partners); and collective procedures for responding to allegations of misconduct. Crucially, the Statement establishes that partners should take collective responsibility for the trustworthiness of the overall collaborative research — not just their own component.

3rd World Conference on Research Integrity, Montréal, 2013 · wcrif.org/montreal-statement · Also available in Bahasa Malaysia

Codes of practice and responsible conduct of research — practical standards

UKRIO Code of Practice for Research

The UK Research Integrity Office Code of Practice sets out standards covering: excellence in research design and conduct; a safe and supportive research environment; transparent and honest communication of results; fair and rigorous peer review; responsible data management; and equitable, transparent authorship. It distinguishes between primary obligations (non-negotiable standards) and aspirational goals (characteristics of excellent research culture). The Code applies to all researchers regardless of career stage.

ukrio.org — Code of Practice

NSF Responsible and Ethical Conduct of Research (RECR)

The US National Science Foundation requires all funded institutions to have a plan for RECR training across all levels — undergraduates, postgraduates, postdoctoral scholars, and senior personnel. RECR covers: conducting peer review with the highest ethical standards; protecting proprietary information and intellectual property; treating students and colleagues fairly and with respect; rigorous and honest generation and dissemination of knowledge. NSF also recognises that research integrity standards and challenges differ across international contexts, and that researchers in cross-national collaborations must be aware of different regulatory environments.

nsf.gov/responsible-research-conduct · International contexts

Researcher integrity commitments — active obligations at every stage of the research cycle

✅ Pre-register hypothesis, design, and primary outcomes before data collection
✅ Keep complete, accurate, auditable research records throughout
✅ Report all pre-specified outcomes — not just statistically significant ones
✅ Apply correct statistical methods and report exact test statistics
✅ Share data and materials openly where possible (FAIR principles)
✅ Apply appropriate authorship criteria — contribution-based, not honorary
✅ Disclose all conflicts of interest financial and non-financial
✅ Acknowledge all sources, funding, and contributions accurately
✅ Correct errors promptly and transparently — issue corrigenda when needed
✅ Report suspected misconduct through appropriate institutional channels
